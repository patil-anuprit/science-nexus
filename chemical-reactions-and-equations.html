📖 Chapter Introduction

Everything around us undergoes change. Milk turns into curd. Iron rusts. Food gets digested. Wood burns. These changes involve chemical reactions. A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances are converted into new substances having different properties. Chemical reactions are responsible for countless natural and man-made processes occurring around us every day.

⭐ Important Definitions

Chemical Reaction

A process in which one or more substances are transformed into new substances with different properties.

Reactants

The substances that take part in a chemical reaction.

Products

The new substances formed after a chemical reaction.

Chemical Equation

A symbolic representation of a chemical reaction using chemical formulae.

🧪 What is a Chemical Reaction?

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances (reactants) are converted into new substances (products) having different chemical properties. During a chemical reaction, atoms rearrange themselves to form new substances. No atoms are created or destroyed; they are simply reorganized. Examples: • Burning of magnesium ribbon • Rusting of iron • Digestion of food • Formation of curd from milk • Photosynthesis in plants

Characteristics of a Chemical Reaction

🌡 Change in Temperature

Heat may be produced or absorbed during a reaction.

🎨 Change in Colour

A substance may change its colour after reaction.

💨 Evolution of Gas

Some reactions release gases such as oxygen, carbon dioxide or hydrogen.

✨ Formation of Precipitate

An insoluble solid may form during the reaction.

💡 Emission of Light

Some reactions produce light energy.

⚖ Chemical Equations

Scientists use chemical equations to represent chemical reactions in a simple and systematic way. A chemical equation shows: • Reactants • Products • Physical states • Conditions of reaction • Relative amounts of substances Example: Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium Oxide Symbolically: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO

Why Chemical Equations Are Important?

Universal Language

Chemists across the world understand reactions through equations.

Compact Representation

Large reactions can be written in a short form.

Scientific Accuracy

Shows exact substances involved in a reaction.

Easy Analysis

Helps study and compare different reactions.

⚖ Balanced Chemical Equations

A chemical equation must obey the Law of Conservation of Mass. According to this law, mass can neither be created nor destroyed during a chemical reaction. Therefore, the number of atoms of each element must be equal on both sides of the equation. Example: Unbalanced Equation: Mg + O₂ → MgO Balanced Equation: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO

Steps for Balancing Chemical Equations

  1. Write the unbalanced equation.
  2. Count atoms on both sides.
  3. Balance one element at a time.
  4. Use coefficients only.
  5. Verify all atoms are balanced.

🔥 Types of Chemical Reactions

Chemical reactions can be classified into different types based on how reactants are converted into products. Understanding these reactions helps us explain many natural and industrial processes occurring around us.

➕ Combination Reaction

A reaction in which two or more substances combine to form a single product. General Form: A + B → AB Example: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO Magnesium combines with oxygen to form magnesium oxide. Real-Life Example: Burning of magnesium ribbon.

💥 Decomposition Reaction

A reaction in which a single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. General Form: AB → A + B Example: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ Calcium Carbonate → Calcium Oxide + Carbon Dioxide Real-Life Example: Limestone decomposition in cement industries.

🔄 Displacement Reaction

A reaction in which a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound. General Form: A + BC → AC + B Example: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu Zinc displaces copper from copper sulphate solution. Real-Life Example: Metal extraction and electroplating processes.

⚡ Double Displacement Reaction

A reaction in which ions are exchanged between two compounds to form new compounds. General Form: AB + CD → AD + CB Example: Na₂SO₄ + BaCl₂ → BaSO₄ + 2NaCl A white precipitate of barium sulphate is formed. Real-Life Example: Water treatment and chemical analysis.

🔴 Oxidation–Reduction (Redox) Reaction

A reaction in which oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously. Oxidation: Addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen. Reduction: Removal of oxygen or addition of hydrogen. Example: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O Copper oxide loses oxygen and gets reduced. Hydrogen gains oxygen and gets oxidized.

🧠 Quick Comparison of Reaction Types

Combination

Many reactants → One product

Decomposition

One reactant → Many products

Displacement

One element replaces another

Double Displacement

Exchange of ions between compounds

Redox

Oxidation and reduction occur together

🌍 Effects of Oxidation in Everyday Life

Oxidation reactions are not limited to laboratories. They occur around us every day and affect our lives in many ways. Some oxidation reactions are useful, while others can damage materials and food products.

🦀 Corrosion

Corrosion is the slow destruction of metals due to the action of air, moisture or chemicals. The most common example is the rusting of iron. Rust is a reddish-brown coating of hydrated iron oxide formed on iron objects. Example: 4Fe + 3O₂ + xH₂O → 2Fe₂O₃·xH₂O Effects: • Weakens metal structures • Damages bridges and vehicles • Causes economic losses

🛡 Prevention of Corrosion

Methods used to prevent corrosion include: • Painting • Greasing and oiling • Galvanization • Alloy formation • Electroplating These methods prevent direct contact between metal and air or moisture.

🍟 Rancidity

Rancidity is the oxidation of fats and oils present in food. It causes unpleasant smell and taste in food items. Examples: • Chips • Butter • Fried foods • Nuts

🥫 Prevention of Rancidity

Rancidity can be prevented by: • Storing food in airtight containers • Refrigeration • Adding antioxidants • Nitrogen flushing in food packets • Keeping food away from sunlight

🧪 Types of Chemical Reactions

Chemical reactions can be classified into different types based on how reactants are converted into products. Understanding these types helps us identify and study chemical changes occurring around us.

1. Combination Reaction

Two or more substances combine to form a single product. General Form: A + B → AB Example: CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ (Quick Lime + Water → Slaked Lime)

2. Decomposition Reaction

A single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. General Form: AB → A + B Example: 2Pb(NO₃)₂ → 2PbO + 4NO₂ + O₂

3. Displacement Reaction

A more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound. General Form: A + BC → AC + B Example: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu

4. Double Displacement Reaction

Two compounds exchange ions to form new compounds. General Form: AB + CD → AD + CB Example: BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ → BaSO₄ + 2NaCl

➕ Combination Reaction

A combination reaction is a reaction in which two or more substances combine to form a single product. Characteristics: • Only one product is formed. • Usually releases heat. • Common in industrial processes. Examples: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO C + O₂ → CO₂ CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂

Real Life Example

Burning magnesium ribbon forms magnesium oxide.

Importance

Used in manufacturing chemicals and construction materials.

➖ Decomposition Reaction

A decomposition reaction is a reaction in which a single compound breaks into two or more simpler substances. Decomposition reactions occur when energy is supplied in the form of heat, light or electricity. Examples: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ 2AgCl → 2Ag + Cl₂ 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂

Thermal Decomposition

Breakdown by heat.

Photolytic Decomposition

Breakdown by sunlight.

Electrolytic Decomposition

Breakdown by electricity.

🔄 Displacement Reactions

A displacement reaction is a chemical reaction in which a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound. Only a more reactive element can displace a less reactive element. General Form: A + BC → AC + B

Example 1

Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu Zinc is more reactive than copper, so zinc displaces copper from copper sulphate solution.

Example 2

Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu Iron is more reactive than copper and displaces it from the solution.

Observation

The blue colour of copper sulphate solution gradually fades and a brown deposit of copper is formed.

Importance

Used in metal extraction, industrial processes and laboratory experiments.

🔁 Double Displacement Reactions

A double displacement reaction is a reaction in which two compounds exchange ions to form two new compounds. General Form: AB + CD → AD + CB

Example

BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ ↓ BaSO₄ + 2NaCl

Precipitation Reaction

A white insoluble precipitate of barium sulphate is formed.

Ion Exchange

Positive and negative ions exchange partners to form new compounds.

Importance

Widely used in chemical analysis and industrial chemistry.

⚡ Oxidation and Reduction Reactions

Many chemical reactions involve the addition or removal of oxygen and hydrogen. Such reactions are called oxidation and reduction reactions. These reactions occur simultaneously and are together known as Redox Reactions. The term "Redox" comes from Reduction + Oxidation.

Oxidation

The addition of oxygen to a substance or the removal of hydrogen from a substance is called oxidation. Example: 2Cu + O₂ → 2CuO Copper gains oxygen and is oxidised.

Reduction

The removal of oxygen from a substance or the addition of hydrogen to a substance is called reduction. Example: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O Copper oxide loses oxygen and is reduced.

🔄 Redox Reactions

A reaction in which oxidation and reduction take place simultaneously is called a redox reaction. Whenever one substance is oxidised, another substance is reduced. Therefore oxidation and reduction always occur together.

Example

CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O Copper oxide loses oxygen and gets reduced. Hydrogen gains oxygen and gets oxidised.

Key Point

Oxidation and reduction cannot occur independently. Both processes happen together in a redox reaction.

Importance

Redox reactions are involved in respiration, photosynthesis, combustion, corrosion and many industrial processes.

Daily Life Examples

Burning fuels, digestion of food, rusting of iron and production of electricity in batteries.

🌍 Effects of Oxidation in Everyday Life

Oxidation reactions are not limited to laboratories. They occur continuously in our surroundings and affect many materials used in daily life. Two important effects of oxidation are: • Corrosion • Rancidity These processes can damage materials and reduce their usefulness.

🔩 Corrosion

The gradual destruction of metals due to reaction with air, moisture or chemicals present in the environment is called corrosion. Corrosion weakens metals and causes economic loss. The most common example of corrosion is rusting of iron.

Rusting of Iron

Iron reacts with oxygen and moisture to form hydrated iron oxide (rust). Rust is a reddish-brown substance that slowly damages iron objects.

Examples of Corrosion

• Rusting of iron • Black coating on silver • Green coating on copper

Harmful Effects

• Weakens structures • Damages vehicles • Increases maintenance cost • Causes loss of valuable metals

Economic Impact

Corrosion causes huge financial losses every year in industries, transportation and construction.

🛡 Prevention of Corrosion

Corrosion can be reduced by preventing the metal from coming into contact with air and moisture.

Painting

A protective layer of paint prevents air and moisture from reaching the metal surface.

Oiling and Greasing

Oil and grease form a protective coating over metal surfaces.

Galvanisation

Iron is coated with a thin layer of zinc to prevent rusting.

Alloying

Metals are mixed with other elements to improve resistance to corrosion. Example: Stainless Steel.

🍟 Rancidity

The oxidation of fats and oils present in food leading to unpleasant smell and taste is called rancidity. Rancid food becomes unfit for consumption. Rancidity is more common in foods containing oils and fats.

Examples

• Chips • Butter • Fried foods • Nuts and oil-rich foods

Cause

Exposure to oxygen in air causes oxidation of fats and oils.

Effects

Food develops bad smell, unpleasant taste and reduced quality.

Importance

Food industries take special measures to prevent rancidity and increase shelf life.

❄ Prevention of Rancidity

Airtight Containers

Prevent contact of food with oxygen.

Refrigeration

Low temperature slows down oxidation reactions.

Nitrogen Flushing

Packets of chips are filled with nitrogen gas to prevent oxidation.

Antioxidants

Special chemicals are added to food products to slow oxidation.
